SNR vs Time vs concentration

This guidance document is intended to help with the question of "will I be able to detect element X at a conventration of Y ppm using the microprobe using analysis method Z (maps, quant analyses, spectra). The recommendations here are based on some tests I did on 250127, and more details can be found in my notes from that session. My general approach to this problem is to calculate the signal-to-noise ratio as SNR = [Net counts] / sqrt(BG counts). When SNR > 3, we can say the analyte is above the detection limit, and when SNR > 10, we can say that the analyte is above the quantification limit. I use the detection limit at the threshold at which "we can see the peak, and the quantification limit as when "maps look ok" and quantitative analyses "turn out well."

Note: We can calculate the change in SNR by increasing the beam current and/or acquisition time using a simple power law where [SNR new] = [SNR old] * sqrt(R), where R = [new counts per analysis]/[old counts per analysis] keeping in mind that the number of counts is proportional to the beam current and acquisition time. So for example, if you increase the beam current by a factor of 10 and cut the count time in half, then R = 10/2 = 5.

Note: Note: A lot of the analyses were done at 10 nA, so that can be thought of as the “default” beam condition for the guiding remarks below.

Case 1: True major elements (>10 wt%)

  • Everything is easy, even at 10 nA. Not really worth going into details.
  • Mapping is fine, even with a 10 ms dwell time. Can probably see features even with a 10 nA beam, but increasing the beam current is recommended.

Case 2: 1s of wt%

  • EDS spectra should be at least 10s if not 30s at 10 nA.
  • For WDS spectra, the acquisition time needs to be at least 100 s, or it can be less with more beam current.
  • Quant analyses should be fine – even with 10s peak + 5s BG at 10 nA.
  • Maps should turn out ok if at least a 100 nA beam AND 50 ms dwell time are used. At these conditions, a 256 x 256 pixel map will take about an hour. Background maps are recommended. Small-area EDS mapping can be done by calculating "Net maps" with the "SmileView" program.

Case 3: 0.1s of wt% (1000s of ppm)

  • EDS spectra need to take at least 100 s at 10 nA, so a higher beam power is advisable. Sum peaks are increasingly problematic.
  • WDS spectra will need to take about 5 minutes unless higher beam power is used.
  • Quant analyses will start getting close to the quantification limit at 10 s peak / 5 s bg. Better increase the acquisition time or acquisition time…
  • WDS Mapping may be possible using the equivalent of a 50 ms dwell time AND 300 nA beam. Background mapes are required.

Case 4: 0.01s of wt% (100s of ppm)

  • EDS spectra won’t work, and sum peaks become highly problematic.
  • WDS spectra will need to take 10s of minutes to around an hour and need at least 100 nA for clear results.
  • WDS quant analyses can be done in 5-10 minutes if a beam current of 100 nA is used. Background curvature becomes significant, so a 1-2 mm BG offset may be preferred.
  • We have never attempted anything like this before! Based on the results below, a 64 x 64 pixel WDS map could be made using the equivalent of a 21 second dwell time and a 100 nA beam, and it would only take 24 hours and it probably wouldn’t look pretty (SNR = 6.5). To double this SNR, something like a 400 nA beam or 4-day analytical session would be needed. Background maps will probably need to be done using another spectrometer and possibly applying a correction to account for differences in count rate between spectrometers.
